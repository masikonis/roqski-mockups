# Brand direction

## Roquemore Skierski

---

## Identity

### Audience frame

This is a B2B site. The primary visitor is a business owner or GC evaluating outside counsel for a $30,000-$50,000+ engagement. Many run a structured pitch process, comparing litigation plans from 3-4 firms before hiring. They arrive from Google, not referrals, so the site must establish credibility from scratch. Every design decision should answer: would this help a sophisticated buyer choose this firm over the alternatives they're also evaluating?

### Emotional anchor

The firm that handles high-stakes litigation like a partner in your business. Capable, responsive, built on modern infrastructure. Three words: **authoritative, modern, invested.**

### The test

> Would a business owner facing a six-figure dispute look at this and trust that this firm can handle it, without needing to be told so?

### Design principles

1. **Substance over decoration.** Content, typography, and spacing carry the design. No boxes, no tiles, no decorative containers. Lines and whitespace separate; nothing decorative is added on top.

2. **Earned authority.** Show capability through evidence (case results, team depth, court experience) rather than claiming it. The work should be visible before any copy tries to summarize it.

3. **Modern infrastructure.** This is a technology-first firm. The site should reflect that: fast, responsive, built to the same standard as the firm's own systems. Not "tech-savvy" as marketing copy, technology as operating reality.

4. **Depth on demand.** On content-heavy pages (practice areas, blog posts, locations), the surface is clean and focused: an overview, a clear value, a next step. SEO content and supporting detail live behind expandable sections. Visitors choose how deep to go. The site never feels cluttered, but nothing is missing.

## Anti-patterns

| Avoid | Why |
|-------|-----|
| Boxes, tiles, and bordered containers for grouping content | Lines, spacing, and typographic hierarchy do the work instead. Hairline-divided grids on a single ground (no per-cell borders, shadows, or fills) are the approved expression of this rule. |
| Hero slideshows rotating full marketing messages | The homepage should deliver one clear, unified message. Single-element typographic cycling (one noun rotating inside one sentence) is the approved hero device. |
| Cliché litigation phrasing: "fight for you," "don't fight solo" | Associated with personal injury firms. The concept of assertiveness is fine; the stock phrasing is not. |
| Google review embeds | Consumer and PI territory. Testimonials should be curated and presented with editorial control. |
| Overly visible content density | Heavy SEO content should be available but not dominate the viewport. Expandable sections keep pages clean. |
| Animation that calls attention to itself | Transitions should feel elegant, not performative. If an effect distracts from the content, it doesn't belong. |
| Consumer conversion patterns: "free consultation," scattered CTAs, urgency language | The audience is evaluating firms in a structured process, not impulse-buying. Pressure tactics undermine the authority positioning. |
| Lifestyle or consumer-oriented hero imagery | The firm serves businesses, not individuals. Hero imagery should reference the professional/commercial context (cityscape, architecture), not personal lifestyle. |

## Conversion approach

One professional contact path, not scattered conversion pressure. A "Contact" link in the navigation leads to a clear contact path with office information, phone numbers, and a form. Practice area sidebars keep the proven contact form. No "free consultation" language. CTAs should frame the firm as a problem-solver ("How can we solve your problem?"), not as a commodity offering free time. Availability claims (for example, "Available 24/7") are acceptable as capability statements in the footer; they are not to be used as CTA pressure.

The homepage form flopped. Do not repeat it. Conversion on the homepage comes from building enough trust and credibility that the visitor navigates to a contact page or practice area on their own.

## Required trust elements

The homepage must establish credibility for a visitor evaluating multiple firms. These are not optional sections:

- **Awards and recognitions.** Prominent placement, similar to the current site's below-hero treatment. The firm actively uses these in sales.
- **Case results.** Evidence of capability in specific, named matters.
- **Testimonials.** Curated, editorially controlled (not Google review embeds). Reference: Smith Law's testimonial block. Presentation is a fading carousel with dot navigation, not static quotes or review widgets.
- **Team depth.** The team approach (partner + attorney + paralegal on every matter) is a key differentiator. The team section should make the firm feel resourced, not small.

## Voice

### Voice character

Confident and direct, measured and authoritative. The firm speaks the way a senior attorney advises a client: clear recommendations, no hedging. Claims are backed by evidence, not adjectives. The partner-in-your-business positioning comes through in how each matter is treated, not in softened language.

### We are / we aren't

| We are | We aren't |
|--------|-----------|
| Direct: we speak authoritatively and make clear recommendations | Vague or hedging: "we can help with many types of disputes" |
| A partner in your business, staffing a full team on every matter | A firm that hands you off after signing |
| Technology-first: responsive, available, leveraging infrastructure for advantage | Traditional or paper-driven, slow to adapt or respond |
| Measured and authoritative, letting results establish credibility | Cliché: stock phrases that could have come from any PI firm |
| Specific about local court knowledge: named courts, named outcomes. Do not stress familiarity with judges — lean on decades of experience practicing in the area | A firm that claims expertise without naming where or how |

### Language signals

**Sounds like us:**
- Strategic counsel for complex commercial matters
- We handle high-stakes litigation with tight timelines
- A team approach to every representation
- Decades of experience practicing in DFW courts
- Built on modern infrastructure

**Doesn't sound like us:**
- We'll fight for your rights
- Don't fight solo
- Aggressive representation *(the concept is fine, but the word is cliché)*

## Directional signals

### Color direction

Dark teal (#0B3030) and burnt orange (#CD4A16) on white. These colors are the brand identity and carry forward unchanged. The redesign makes them feel current through presentation, not by replacing them. Supporting grounds: off-white (#FAF9F7) for alternate sections, and teal (#0B3030) as a dark ground for high-contrast moments (case results, testimonials, hero).

### Typography direction

Headings use Source Serif 4 (600 to 700). Body uses Plus Jakarta Sans (400 to 600). The serif establishes authority and signals experience; the sans body reads cleanly at length, which matters because practice area pages and blog posts are content-heavy. The type system creates clear hierarchy on its own, since the design doesn't rely on containers to organize content.

### Imagery direction

Founder headshots on a matched background. Dallas architecture for environmental moments (hero, testimonials). A neutral interior scene for section breaks between content blocks. AI-generated imagery is acceptable for generic use.

### Motion direction

Elegant and deliberate, not distracting. Transitions and visual effects should feel refined. Anything that pulls attention from the content is over the line. Continuous, content-carrying motion (for example, a logo marquee) is acceptable when slow, uninterrupted, and pausable on hover; decorative or attention-seeking motion is not. The specific implementation (what animates, how, where) belongs in visual exploration, not here.
